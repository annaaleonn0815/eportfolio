Module Artefacts

Below are the various artefacts I have created throughout the Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) module. This is evidenced through my completion of Codio exercises, literature reviews, and collaborative discussions. These works have been important in helping me achieve the learning outcomes of this module; learning concepts such as encapsulation, recursion, and polymorphism is important because they are essential concepts in object-oriented programming. These concepts help future software developers such as myself write code that is modular, reusable, and efficient. Learning these specific concepts through the creation of these artefacts allowed me to design and implement programs that showcase the appropriate use of object-oriented design concepts (Learning Outcome 2). Participating in discussions on reusability and IoT systems has also helped me critically evaluate and appraise the strengths and weaknesses in object-oriented programming in comparison to other approaches (Learning Outcome 1). Similarly, creating various UML and coding scripts has taught me to use the appropriate data structures in any given problem (Learning Outcomes 3 & 4). Overall, creating artefacts throughout the module has helped provide insight as to how I can apply object-oriented programming principles in any future code that I write, as well as appropriately assess the reusability of my code.

Collaborative Discussion 1 Summary In Collaborative Discussion 1, I discussed the article by Padhy et al. (2018) on the reusability of object-oriented software and identified the 6 most important factors that influence its reusability. These include the requirement analysis, knowledge requirement, algorithms used in the program, architecture-driven approach, documentation in the project, and design patterns. I also listed five factors as the least important factors influencing the reusability of object-oriented software, including the modules used in the data project, modules in the project and program, test cases, and service contracts. I found this discussion to be important because it made me gain valuable insights as to what I can prioritize as both a student and future software developer to make my programs reusable, efficient, and cost-effective. This discussion encouraged me to think beyond coding itself and consider in a broader context in which my software will be used. Feedback from peers, My peer responses
Collaborative Discussion Post 2 Summary In Collaborative Discussion 2, I discussed the benefits of designing a metamodel to support the development of smart car, as well as object-oriented IoT systems. I was able to highlight the strengths of metamodeling, such as reducing the computational burden, simplifying the design process, and enabling collaboration through standardization. I also identified some of the weaknesses, such as the complexity of the metamodel and the difficulty in achieving standardization. This discussion is important because understanding the strengths and weaknesses of software development approaches is crucial for future software developers such as myself. Being aware of the strengths of metamodeling would allow developers to recognize how to create more efficient models, while at the same time being aware of the drawbacks.
